Morocco

DEC 2025

A camel convoy carving paths through the crests of the world's greatest desert, the Sahara.

I am watching the sun die over the Great Sahara for the third twilight in a row. Fouad and I grapple over a sun-faded star map, passing the parchment back and forth in a frantic little tug-of-war. With the last lingering drops of daylight, we fiercely memorize the positions of the ink spots on the page. We are looking for an echo in the dark - hoping to find our bearings in the celestial negative that sweeps open above us the moment the light fades.

"Navigating by night is always easier." Fouad flicks these English words as ash from a cigarette. "You'll see."

"We won't see anything, that's my point!"

To trace the path backward from these dunes: it started eight days ago. I had just crossed the water from Malaga, arriving at the port of Melilla. I stepped out from the sway of the boat and onto the gangplank, the North African sun hitting me like a judgement.

The long and winding road to Marrakesh.

To understand the ground we were traveling, you must understand the sheer scale of the kingdom, home to the lives of nearly thirty-four million people. Its identity is scattered across a labyrinth of legendary cities - from the administrative elegance of Rabat to the sprawling, cinematic grandeur of Casablanca; from the northern winds of Tangier and Nador to the imperial echoes of Fes, Meknes, and Marrakesh. But perhaps its most defining feature is not its geography, but its defiance. Uniquely unyielding, Morocco is a historic titan that has preserved a fiercely guarded independence its neighbors have rarely known.

The character of this country was born of a deep, historical defiance. Since the late eighth century, when Idris I founded the first Moroccan state, a succession of autonomous dynasties has charted the nation's course. At its peak, the kingdom was an intercontinental colossus; the Almoravid and Almohad dynasties ruled a vast domain that spanned the Iberian Peninsula and the whole of Northwestern Africa. When the threat of foreign subjugation loomed, the Marinids and Saadis mounted a relentless defense of their borders. It is a testament to this enduring resilience that Morocco remains the great exception of its region - the only North African nation to never bow to Ottoman occupation.

The current sovereigns, the Alaouite dynasty, seized the crown in 1666, steering the nation through its most modern trials. When Europe sought to draw lines across the globe in 1912, Morocco was momentarily fractured - divided between French and Spanish rule, with Tangier left adrift as an international zone. But a nation built on centuries of sovereignty does not easily submit, and independence was restored in 1956. The true triumph of this history is found in the streets today. Moroccan culture is not just a remnant of the past, it is a brilliant confluence of Arab grace, ancient Berber grit, Sub-Saharan vitality, and European influence, woven together over centuries of sun and sand.

A stall at the Jemaa El Fnaa street market.

The story of modern Morocco cannot be told without acknowledging the weight of its southern frontier. Morocco lays claim to the contested territory of Western Sahara, referring to it officially as its Southern Provinces - a designation not universally recognized. The annexation in 1975 set off a protracted guerilla conflict with the indigenous Sahrawi population, a war that smoldered for years until a tentative cease-fire was reached in 1991. Yet peace remains only a word on paper. Successive negotiations have faltered, and the political impasse persists, unbroken and unresolved, suspended in the same vast uncertainty as the desert sky.

Morocco stands as a constitutional monarchy, governed by both crown and elected parliament. Yet make no mistake - true authority resides with the King. He holds sweeping executive and legislative power, commanding the armed forces, directing foreign policy, and serving as the guardian of religious life. Governance is nominally shared between the government and two parliamentary assemblies: the Assembly of Representatives and the Assembly of Councillors. But the King's will is supreme. Through royal decrees called dahirs, his word becomes the law. And should the political balance falter, he possesses the unilateral power to dissolve parliament, bound only by brief consultations with the Prime Minister and the President of the Constitutional Court.

Morocco is a nation shaped by faith. Islam is not merely the predominant religion, it is the foundation upon which daily life is built. The official languages - Arabic and Tamazight - speak to both the Arab and indigenous Berber heritage; however, in the bustling streets you are far more likely to hear Darija, the Moroccan Arabic dialect, or French, inherited from decades of colonial presence. Politically and economically, Morocco is no minor player. It holds considerable sway as a member of the Arab League and a key participant in the Union for the Mediterranean. With the fifth-largest economy in Africa, it is a nation that has learned to navigate the complexities of modernity without abandoning the traditions that define it.

When the sun surrenders, the desert becomes a place of haunting, quiet beauty.
